# Application Portfolio Rationalization
## Glossary of Terms

**Version:** 1.0
**Last Updated:** November 2024

---

## A

**APM (Application Performance Monitoring)**
Tools and processes used to monitor the performance and availability of software applications, including metrics like response time, throughput, and error rates.

**Application Portfolio**
The complete collection of software applications owned and operated by an organization, including custom-built, commercial off-the-shelf (COTS), and cloud/SaaS applications.

**Application Rationalization**
The process of evaluating an organization's application portfolio to identify opportunities for consolidation, retirement, modernization, or investment to optimize business value and reduce costs.

---

## B

**BEC (Business Email Compromise)**
A type of fraud where attackers impersonate executives or vendors to trick employees into making unauthorized payments or sharing sensitive information.

**Business Criticality**
A measure of how essential an application is to business operations, typically rated as Critical, High, Medium, or Low based on the impact of unavailability.

---

## C

**CMDB (Configuration Management Database)**
A database that stores information about hardware and software assets, their configurations, and relationships, used by IT organizations to manage infrastructure.

**Consolidation**
The process of combining multiple applications that serve similar functions into a single solution to reduce redundancy, costs, and complexity.

---

## D

**Data Classification**
The process of categorizing data based on its sensitivity and the impact of unauthorized disclosure, typically using labels like Public, Internal, Confidential, and Restricted.

**Disposition**
The recommended action for an application based on rationalization analysis, such as Tolerate, Invest, Migrate, or Eliminate (TIME framework).

---

## E

**End of Life (EOL)**
The date when a software vendor stops supporting a product, including security updates, bug fixes, and technical support.

**Enterprise Architecture**
A discipline that describes an organization's structure, processes, and technology, providing a blueprint for aligning IT investments with business strategy.

---

## F

**FTE (Full-Time Equivalent)**
A unit of measurement equal to one person working full-time, used to quantify staffing levels and resource allocation.

---

## G

**Governance**
The framework of policies, processes, and decision rights that guide how applications are acquired, maintained, and retired within an organization.

---

## I

**Integration**
The connection between two or more applications that allows them to share data or functionality, including APIs, file transfers, and direct database connections.

---

## K

**KPI (Key Performance Indicator)**
A measurable value that demonstrates how effectively an organization is achieving key business objectives.

---

## L

**Legacy Application**
An older application that may use outdated technology but remains in use because it performs a critical function or is too costly to replace.

---

## M

**MCC (Merchant Category Code)**
A four-digit number used to classify businesses by the type of goods or services they provide, used in fraud detection for transaction monitoring.

**Migration**
The process of moving an application from one platform, technology, or environment to another, such as moving from on-premise to cloud.

**Modernization**
The process of updating an application's technology stack, architecture, or user interface to improve performance, security, or maintainability.

---

## O

**On-Premise**
Software that is installed and runs on computers on the premises of the organization using the software, rather than at a remote facility such as a cloud provider.

---

## P

**Portfolio Analysis**
The process of evaluating a collection of applications across multiple dimensions such as business value, technical health, cost, and risk to inform investment decisions.

---

## R

**ROI (Return on Investment)**
A measure of the profitability of an investment, calculated as (Net Benefit / Cost) x 100, used to justify rationalization initiatives.

---

## S

**SaaS (Software as a Service)**
A software distribution model where applications are hosted by a provider and made available to customers over the internet, typically on a subscription basis.

**Stakeholder**
An individual or group with an interest in an application, including business owners, technical owners, end users, and executives.

**SAR (Suspicious Activity Report)**
A document that financial institutions must file with regulatory authorities when they detect known or suspected violations of law or suspicious activity.

---

## T

**TCO (Total Cost of Ownership)**
The complete cost of owning and operating an application over its lifetime, including licensing, infrastructure, support, training, and opportunity costs.

**Technical Debt**
The implied cost of additional work caused by choosing an easy or quick solution now instead of a better approach that would take longer.

**TIME Framework**
A disposition framework for application rationalization with four categories:
- **Tolerate**: Maintain with minimal investment
- **Invest**: Enhance, modernize, or scale
- **Migrate**: Move to new platform or replace
- **Eliminate**: Retire, decommission, or consolidate

---

## U

**Use Case**
A specific scenario describing how a user interacts with an application to achieve a goal, used to document requirements and test functionality.

---

## V

**Vendor Lock-in**
A situation where a customer is dependent on a vendor for products and services and cannot easily move to another vendor without substantial costs or inconvenience.

---

## W

**Weighted Scoring**
A method of evaluating options by assigning importance weights to criteria and calculating a composite score based on how each option performs against those criteria.
